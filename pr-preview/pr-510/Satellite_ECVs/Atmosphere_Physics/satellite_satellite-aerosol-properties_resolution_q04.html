1.1.1. Assessing the consistency of OLCI and SLSTR Sentinel-3A AOD retrievals during the Hunga Tonga volcanic eruption#

 

Production date: 30-09-2024

Produced by: University of Salerno (UNISA- Faezeh Karimian and Fabio Madonna)

🌍 Use case: Investigating volcanic eruptions using satellite observations#

❓ Quality assessment question#

• How consistent are the OLCI and SLSTR Sentinel-3A daily AOD550 retrievals in monitoring the spatial and temporal evolution of volcanic aerosols during the Hunga Tonga eruption in January 2022?

Volcanic eruptions, such as the January 2022 Hunga Tonga–Hunga Haʻapai event, inject large quantities of ash and sulfate aerosols into the upper troposphere and lower stratosphere. These particles disperse over vast distances and modify the radiative balance of the atmosphere [1], [2], producing a volcanic plume detectable by multiple satellite sensors. Reliable monitoring of such extreme aerosol events requires high consistency between different satellite retrieval products.

This notebook evaluates the consistency of daily aerosol optical depth (AOD550) retrievals from the Copernicus Climate Change Service (C3S) OLCI and SLSTR Sentinel-3A aerosol products during the eruption period. The C3S aerosol Climate Data Record provides four retrieval products for each sensor: ORAC, SU, SDV, and ENS. While ORAC, SU, and SDV are generated using independent aerosol retrieval algorithms, the ENS product combines them to provide a consolidated ensemble estimate of aerosol optical depth. This analysis specifically utilizes the OLCI Sentinel-3A ENS version 1.1 and the SLSTR Sentinel-3A ENS version 2.3 products.

Rather than focusing on the volcanic event itself as a case study, the primary objective is to assess whether the OLCI and SLSTR Sentinel-3A ENS products provide consistent information regarding the spatial and temporal evolution of aerosol loading. The analysis spans from 13 to 21 January 2022, encompassing the main eruption and the initial days of plume development.

📢 Quality assessment statement#

These are the key outcomes of this assessment

This assessment evaluates the consistency of the C3S ENS AOD550 products derived from OLCI and SLSTR Sentinel-3A observations during the Hunga Tonga volcanic eruption (13–21 January 2022).

  • Both products (OLCI and SLSTR Sentinel-3A) capture the temporal evolution of enhanced aerosol loading following the eruption.

  • Spatial agreement is generally high, with daily correlations ranging from 0.68 to 0.94 for most days and a mean correlation of 0.74.

  • Mean bias remains close to zero (0.003), indicating no strong systematic difference between the products.

  • Differences are mainly associated with retrieval coverage and periods of intense aerosol loading.

  • Overall, the OLCI and SLSTR ENS products provide consistent information on the evolution of volcanic aerosols at 1° spatial resolution during the analysed period.

📋 Methodology#

This assessment evaluates the consistency of daily AOD550 retrievals from the C3S ENS aerosol products derived from the OLCI and SLSTR instruments onboard Sentinel-3A during the Hunga Tonga–Hunga Ha’apai volcanic eruption. The objective is to determine whether both products provide comparable information on the spatial and temporal evolution of volcanic aerosols during and after the eruption.

The analysis uses daily ENS AOD550 products from OLCI Sentinel-3A version 1.1 and SLSTR Sentinel-3A version 2.3 between 13 and 21 January 2022, covering the eruption and the first days of plume development. The ENS product combines retrievals from the ORAC, SU and SDV aerosol algorithms into a consolidated ensemble estimate designed to reduce algorithm-specific biases and provide a more robust aerosol retrieval.

The analysis is restricted to a domain extending from 40°S to 5°S and from 180°W to 145°W, encompassing the Hunga Tonga volcano and the main region affected by the initial eastward transport of volcanic aerosols. This domain was selected to capture both the source region and the early plume evolution while maintaining a sufficient number of collocated retrievals for quantitative inter-product comparison.

To assess consistency between OLCI and SLSTR, both qualitative and quantitative analyses are performed. First, daily AOD550 maps and animations are generated for both products to examine whether they identify similar aerosol structures and temporal changes following the eruption. An animation provides a visual assessment of the spatial evolution of the volcanic plume and highlights similarities and differences in retrieval coverage and aerosol patterns.

Second, the temporal evolution of elevated aerosol loading is evaluated using daily maximum AOD550 values and daily 99th percentile AOD550 values. The maximum AOD550 highlights the strongest aerosol signal detected each day, while the 99th percentile provides a more robust indicator of high aerosol loading that is less sensitive to isolated extreme pixels. Consistent temporal behaviour between the products supports a consistency of both datasets in capturing the evolution of the volcanic plume in a similar manner.

Third, pixel-by-pixel comparisons are performed using collocated valid observations available in both products. Only grid cells containing valid AOD550 retrievals from both OLCI and SLSTR are included in the comparison. For each day, the Pearson correlation coefficient, mean bias and root mean square error (RMSE) are calculated. The correlation coefficient is used to assess the similarity of spatial patterns, while bias and RMSE quantify systematic and overall differences between the products.

The consistency assessment is based on several verification steps before interpreting the results. Since no independent validation datasets, such as sun photometer or ground-based lidar observations, are used, the analysis relies on internal consistency between spatial maps, temporal evolution metrics, and pixel-level agreement statistics.

Finally, summary statistics are calculated across all analysed days to provide an overall assessment of product consistency during the eruption period. These summary metrics are used to evaluate whether the OLCI and SLSTR ENS products provide comparable information on volcanic aerosol evolution at 1° spatial resolution.

def animate_two_products_inline(ds1, ds2, var, label1, label2, vmin=0, vmax=1):
    
    data1 = ds1[var]
    data2 = ds2[var]
    
    fig, axes = plt.subplots(
        1, 2,
        figsize=(14, 5),
        subplot_kw={"projection": ccrs.PlateCarree()}
    )
    
    def update(i):
        for ax in axes:
            ax.clear()
            ax.coastlines()
            ax.set_extent([-180, -140, -40, 0], crs=ccrs.PlateCarree())
            ax.plot(
                VOLCANO["lon"], VOLCANO["lat"],
                marker="*", color="cyan", markersize=12,
                transform=ccrs.PlateCarree()
            )
        
        date = pd.to_datetime(data1.time.values[i]).strftime("%Y-%m-%d")
        
        im1 = data1.isel(time=i).plot(
            ax=axes[0],
            transform=ccrs.PlateCarree(),
            cmap="inferno",
            vmin=vmin,
            vmax=vmax,
            add_colorbar=False
        )
        axes[0].set_title(f"{label1} {var} — {date}")
        
        im2 = data2.isel(time=i).plot(
            ax=axes[1],
            transform=ccrs.PlateCarree(),
            cmap="inferno",
            vmin=vmin,
            vmax=vmax,
            add_colorbar=False
        )
        axes[1].set_title(f"{label2} {var} — {date}")
        
        return im1, im2
    
    anim = FuncAnimation(
        fig,
        update,
        frames=len(data1.time),
        interval=1000,
        blit=False
    )
    
    cbar_ax = fig.add_axes([0.92, 0.18, 0.015, 0.65])
    sm = plt.cm.ScalarMappable(
        cmap="inferno",
        norm=plt.Normalize(vmin=vmin, vmax=vmax)
    )
    fig.colorbar(sm, cax=cbar_ax, label=var)
    
    plt.close(fig)
    
    display(HTML(anim.to_jshtml()))
    
    return anim

Calculation of pixel-level agreement metrics#

To quantify the consistency between OLCI and SLSTR AOD550 retrievals, a pixel-by-pixel comparison is performed for each common observation date. Only collocated grid cells containing valid retrievals in both products are included in the analysis. Three complementary agreement metrics are calculated: the Pearson correlation coefficient, which measures the similarity of spatial aerosol patterns; the mean bias (SLSTR − OLCI), which indicates systematic differences between the products; and the root mean square error (RMSE), which quantifies the overall magnitude of retrieval differences. The number of valid collocated pixels is also reported for each day to provide context for the robustness of the statistical comparison.

def daily_agreement(ds1, ds2, var):

    rows = []

    for t in ds1.time.values:

        a = ds1[var].sel(time=t)
        b = ds2[var].sel(time=t)

        mask = np.isfinite(a.values) & np.isfinite(b.values)

        if mask.sum() < 3:
            r = np.nan
            p = np.nan
            bias = np.nan
            rmse = np.nan
            n = mask.sum()

        else:
            a_valid = a.values[mask]
            b_valid = b.values[mask]

            r, p = pearsonr(a_valid, b_valid)

            bias = np.mean(b_valid - a_valid)
            rmse = np.sqrt(
                np.mean((b_valid - a_valid) ** 2)
            )

            n = mask.sum()

        rows.append({
            "date": pd.to_datetime(t),
            "variable": var,
            "correlation": r,
            "p_value": p,
            "bias_SLSTR_minus_OLCI": bias,
            "rmse": rmse,
            "n_valid_pixels": n
        })

    return pd.DataFrame(rows)

The statistical indicators reinforce the qualitative findings derived from the visual analysis. The Pearson correlation coefficients demonstrate high sensor agreement during the initial phase of the event, with values exceeding 0.9. However, the notable decrease in correlation, coupled with a peak in RMSE (0.066) on January 18th, highlights the sensors’ divergent performance during the peak of the volcanic activity. The persistent, albeit small, positive bias (SLSTR–OLCI) further confirms that SLSTR consistently reports higher AOD values than OLCI across most days. These metrics suggest that while the sensors track the general plume evolution in unison, their quantitative retrievals become increasingly decoupled under high aerosol loading conditions, likely due to differences in algorithm sensitivities to the unique optical properties of the volcanic ash and sulfate plume.

4. Take-home messages#

  • While both sensors capture the plume’s progression, OLCI exhibits higher AOD values and sharper peaks compared to SLSTR retrieval.

  • Despite a generally good average spatial correlation (mean 0.74), performance degrades significantly during peak activity, where lower correlations and increased RMSE (up to 0.07) indicate substantial decoupling between the two products.

  • Minimal Bias, High Variance: While the overall mean bias is negligible (0.003), this statistic masks the sensor disagreement observed during high aerosol loading events, where individual instrument characteristics lead to divergent estimations of plume intensity.

  • The OLCI and SLSTR products offer complementary data for monitoring volcanic events, but their systematic discrepancies under dense aerosol conditions necessitate caution. Users should be aware that neither sensor provides a definitive characterization, and their combined use is essential to mitigate the uncertainties inherent in individual instrument retrievals.
