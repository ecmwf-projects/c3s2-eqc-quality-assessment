1.1.1. Cloud type determination for climate studies#

Production date, after final revision: 02-06-2026

Producer : CNRS/LMD-IPSL (Sullivan Carbone, Claudia Stubenrauch, Giulio Mandorli) ; UVSQ/LSCE-IPSL (Hélène Brogniez)

🌍 Use case: Monitoring cloud amount and relative occurrence of specific cloud types#

❓ Quality assessment question#

  • How well are the decadal changes in high-level cloud types represented in the CLARA-A3 satellite dataset ?

Clouds are fundamental regulators of the Earth’s climate system, significantly influencing the hydrological cycle, the atmospheric circulation and the Earth’s heat balance. These factors, in turn, play a crucial role in shaping ecosystems, agriculture, water resources, and weather patterns. The overall impact of clouds on Earth’s climate is determined by their physical properties, including their amount, their height, and their opacity (how much the cloud blocks sunlight and traps heat). The following notebook essentially focuses on the occurrence of high-level clouds, easiest observed from space, and which have important atmospheric radiative effects: they strongly trap outgoing longwave radiation from the Earth’s surface. While high opaque clouds strongly reflect solar radiation, thin cirrus allow most solar radiation to pass. The net radiative effect of high-level clouds therefore depends on their opacity, given by the optical thickness. Furthermore, a change in the geographical patterns of their relative occurrence leads to changes in the atmospheric circulation and thus in precipitation patterns.

This analysis uses cloud observations from the dataset Cloud properties global gridded monthly and daily data from 1979 to present derived from satellite observations, available in the Climate Data Store (CDS). Two complementary Thematic Climate Data Records of cloud properties are provided by two product families: CLARA-A3 (CMSAF cLoud, Albedo and surface Radiation) based on measurements by the Advanced Very High Resolution Radiometers (AVHRR), and CCI (Climate Change Initiative), based on measurements by the (Advanced) Along-Track Scanning Radiometers ((A)ATSR). The latter record has been extended in time using measurements by the Sea and Land Surface Temperature Radiometers (SLSTR). The data of these two product families differ in temporal sampling, horizontal resolution and methods to retrieve cloud properties.

In order to define cloud types according to height and opacity, this notebook uses Joint Cloud Histograms (JCH). These provide monthly cloud occurrence statistics within 13 x 15 two-dimensional intervals in Cloud Optical Thickness (COT) and Cloud-Top Pressure (CTP), averaged over grid cells of 1° latitude x 1° longitude. At the moment of creating this notebook, the CDS provides JCHs only for CLARA-A3. When JCHs are also available for CCI, these can be used similarly.

This notebook gives examples how these JCHs can be used to study the relative occurrence of specific cloud types. We first compare the geographical cloud type distributions of CLARA-A3 with those obtained from other cloud retrievals. Then, the interannual variability of the relative occurrence of three different high-level cloud types (high opaque, cirrus and thin cirrus) is shown in the tropics, and finally we explore their geographical anomaly patterns with respect to global surface temperature changes.

In this notebook, we define high-level clouds as clouds with a cloud-top pressure < 310 hPa. This corresponds to a height of about 10 km in the tropics and 8.5 - 9 km in the midlatitudes. They are then further distinguished as thin cirrus, cirrus and high opaque clouds, using COT thresholds of 1.3 and 9.4, respectively. This cloud type identification corresponds to the one applied in the updated Global Energy and Water Exchanges (GEWEX) cloud assessment [3], which has recently inter-compared 11 publicly available global cloud data records derived from measurements of multi-spectral imagers, IR sounders, and lidar.

The CLARA-A3 cloud detection relies on a Bayesian approach, by linking measurements from all AVHRR channels to the cloud detection from the Cloud-Aerosol Lidar and Infrared Pathfinder Satellite Observation mission (CALIPSO)[1] as a reliable reference. CTP is retrieved based on a neural network approach, trained on CALIPSO cloud-top height observations. In a second step, it is transformed to cloud-top height and temperature by applying atmospheric profiles from meteorological reanalyses. Ice clouds are distinguished from liquid clouds by considering spectral infrared brightness temperature differences. COT is only retrieved during daytime, using look-up tables (LUTs) based on visible and near-infrared reflectances and radiative transfer [2]. Therefore, the JCHs are also only available during daytime.

📢 Quality assessment statement#

It is important to note that passive remote sensing has limitations: it provides a top-down perspective, which does not account for hidden cloud layers, and classical retrievals, based on radiative transfer, estimate a ‘radiative’ cloud height. This radiative height can be several kilometers below the actual cloud top, especially in cases where clouds have diffuse tops, with optical depth gradually increasing from the top down over a significant vertical range. This phenomenon is relevant for approximately 70% of high clouds in the tropics and 30-40% of high clouds in the midlatitudes [4].

To overcome this problem, recent retrievals, such as the one developed for CLARA-A3, directly obtain the cloud-top height by using neural networks which have been trained on the cloud-top height derived by active lidar (CALIOP).

One has also to keep in mind that the retrieved optical thickness depends on the footprint of the initial measurement, since for the cloud property retrieval the whole footprint is assumed to be completely cloud-covered. Therefore, smaller instrument footprints should in general lead to larger Cloud Optical Thickness. This has an influence on the relative distribution between high opaque, cirrus and thin cirrus clouds.

These are the key outcomes of this assessment

  • Over the tropics, according to CLARA-A3, high-level clouds make up about 55% of all clouds over ocean and 65% over land. These values are in agreement with active lidar retrievals by the CALIPSO Science Team. However, it is worth to be noted that active lidar is more sensitive to thin cirrus clouds than passive instruments.

  • When CLARA-A3 classifies these tropical high-level clouds by opacity, over ocean (land) about 20 (30) % of them are opaque, while 45 (55) % of them are cirrus clouds and 35 (15) % of them are thin cirrus clouds. These results are similar to the ones retrieved from the MODIS continuity retrieval. In contrast, other satellite datasets do not exhibit a comparable land-ocean contrast in thin cirrus coverage, suggesting that thin cirrus over land is still underestimated in CLARA-A3 due to persistent difficulty of detecting optically thin clouds over bright and heterogeneous land surfaces.

  • The tropical annual mean time series over the whole period (1980-2020) are only stable for maritime high opaque clouds. For other high-level cloud types, stable records only exist for 2003–2015, when observation times are coherent, with two daytime measurements, at around 10 AM and 1:30 PM local time. Outside this period, changes in observation times reduce temporal stability. However, even over the coherent observation time period 2003-2015, CLARA-A3 shows a relative increase of all high-level cloud types, which is not confirmed by other cloud data records. The present data provide statistics merged over all available daytime observation times. For monitoring purposes, using single consistent overpasses (e.g., 1:30 PM ±1 h) is easier to interprete.

  • During the period of consistent observation times (2003-2015), spatial patterns of cloud type changes in response to surface temperature anomalies show increases near the equator in the tropical Pacific and decreases over Indonesia, consistent with El Niño–Southern Oscillation (ENSO) effects. Thin cirrus amounts also increase near the equator alongside high opaque clouds, reflecting the growth of tropical mesoscale convective systems and their surrounding cirrus. These results are in agreement with the ones found by using other cloud datasets.

def reindex(ds, cot_slices, ctp_slices, lon_slice, lat_slice):
    """
    Reprocess and reindex a 2D COT–CTP histogram dataset over a selected region.

    This function:
    1. Extracts a geographical subset of the dataset.
    2. Aggregates histograms statistics of the original 13x15 (COT, CTP) intervals into 3 x 3 intervals,
       defined by "cot_slices" and "ctp_slices".
    3. Merges the histogram statistics of the two thermodynamic phases into a single aggregate.
    4. Reconstructs labeled COT and CTP dimensions.
    5. Reindexes time into a (year, month) multi-index and unstacks it.

    Parameters
    ----------
    ds : xarray.Dataset
        Input dataset containing hist2d COT–CTP data.
    cot_slices : list of slice
        List of COT bin groupings.
    ctp_slices : list of slice
        List of CTP bin groupings.
    lon_slice : slice
        Longitude bounds for regional selection.
    lat_slice : slice
        Latitude bounds for regional selection.

    Returns
    -------
    xarray.Dataset
        Aggregated and reindexed dataset with reduced COT–CTP resolution
        and time structured as (year, month).
    """
    ds = utils.regionalise(ds, lat_slice=lat_slice, lon_slice=lon_slice)
    datasets = []
    for cot_index, cot_slice in enumerate(cot_slices):
        for ctp_index, ctp_slice in enumerate(ctp_slices):
            ds_limit = ds.sel(
                hist2d_cot_bin_centre=cot_slice,
                hist2d_ctp_bin_centre=ctp_slice,
            )
            ds_limit = ds_limit.sum(
                ["hist_phase", "hist2d_ctp_bin_centre", "hist2d_cot_bin_centre"],
                keep_attrs=True,
            )
            ds_limit = ds_limit.expand_dims(
                COT=[infer_label(cot_slice, "COT")],
                CTP=[infer_label(ctp_slice, "CTP", "hPa")],
            )
            ds_limit = ds_limit.assign_coords(
                cot_index=("COT", [cot_index]), ctp_index=("CTP", [ctp_index])
            )
            datasets.append(ds_limit)
    ds = xr.combine_by_coords(datasets)
    ds = ds.sortby(["cot_index", "ctp_index"]).drop_vars(["cot_index", "ctp_index"])
    ds = ds.drop_dims(["hist2d_cot_bin_border", "hist2d_ctp_bin_border"])

    # Time reindex
    ds = ds.assign_coords(
        year=("time", ds["time"].dt.year.astype(int).values),
        month=("time", ds["time"].dt.month.astype(int).values),
    )
    ds = ds.set_index(time=("year", "month")).unstack("time")
    return ds

The comparison of Figure 1 with the maps of four datasets presented in Figure 10 of [3], and extracted as Figure 2 in this notebook, leads to the following assessment of CLARA-A3: Compared to CLARA-A2 (V2.0), CLARA-A3 provides more high-level and less mid-level clouds. This can be easily explained by the fact that the CLARA-A3 neural network retrieval, trained on lidar data, estimates the cloud top, while the classical CLARA-A2 retrieval estimates the lower ‘radiative’ cloud height. Another difference can be noticed for the Stratocumulus clouds off the Western coasts, which seem to have a more structured occurrence, again due to the new neural network approach linked to lidar height.

The geographical patterns of the normalized occurrence of tropical thin cirrus given by CLARA-A3 are similar to the ones given by AIRS-CIRS [6], though with less thin cirrus over land. This shows an improvement compared to CLARA-A2, as IR sounders such as AIRS identify thin cirrus reliably.

The geographical patterns of cirrus are similar to those of PATMOS-x [7] using the same AVHRR measurements, MODIS-CLDPROP [8] using the MODIS Science Team cloud property continuity retrieval, and AIRS-CIRS, with maxima of cirrus anvils above the most convective regions, above Indonesia, South America and Africa. However, CLARA-A3 presents more of these higher thicker clouds in the midlatitudes. This can be explained again by the fact that the neural network retrieval places the high-level clouds close to the tropopause, while the retrievals based on radiative transfer place the clouds lower (see also the notebook ‘Intercomparison of cloud-top pressure distributions derived from satellite observations’).

Interannual variability#

The JCHs also include the cloud area fraction for each 1° grid cell. Since the JCHs include only cloudy statistics, this variable can be used as a weight in order to transform relative occurrences into absolute occurrences. In the following, we consider time series of total cloud amount and relative cloud type amounts, separately over ocean and over land.

The upper panels of Figure 3 present time series of the tropical annual mean cloud amount (CA), computed as the average over the latitude band 25N-25S of the annual mean fractional cloud cover per 1° grid. These curves can be directly compared to the results of the updated GEWEX cloud assessment presented in Figure 4.

The time series of tropical CA is more stable over land than over ocean, the latter showing two significant increases in the early 1990’s and in 2002. These increases over ocean also appear in the tropical CA of PATMOS-x, while other data records, including CLARA-A2, do not show this behaviour. This can be explained by the fact that PATMOS-x and CLARA-A3 use a similar cloud detection method. The tropical CA of CLARA-A3 over ocean is about 0.1 higher than over land, in agreement with AIRS-CIRS, CALIPSO-ST (retrieval by CALIPSO Science Team) and MODIS-CE (retrieval by MODIS CERES Science Team, [15]. However, while CLARA-A3 and CLARA-A2 (V2.1) provide a similar cloud cover in the tropical latitude bands in the notebook ‘Satellite cloud cover validation for Climate modelling’, the cloud amount of CLARA-A3 over tropical land seems to be slightly underestimated, with about 0.4, compared to about 0.5 from CLARA-A2 (V2.0) and all other retrievals using AVHRR measurements. It is interesting to note that the CA over tropical land of CALIPSO-ST_passive, which simulates a CA of clouds with COT > 0.3, is also only 0.4. This indicates that CLARA-A3 has problems to detect / identify thin clouds over land.

The lower panels of Figure 3 present time series of the tropical annual mean amount of all high-level clouds (blue), as well as of thin cirrus (orange), cirrus (green) and high opaque clouds (red), relative to all clouds, again separately over ocean and over land. The threshold of 310 hPa corresponds in the tropics to a height of about 10 km. The relative amount of high-level clouds from CLARA-A3 is relatively large and compares well with the one of CALIPSO-ST, with about 60% and 70% respectively. However, the interannual variability has not the same behaviour. It was already pointed out in [3] that it is very difficult to observe cloud trends within latitudinal bands using currently available cloud data records. Furthermore, the AVHRR cloud products, existing over a 40-year period, are more stable after 2003. The CLARA-A3 time series also show differences in behaviour between the periods before and after 2003. This can be explained by the observation times which have been used to create the CLARA-A3 JCHs: Only during the period 2003-2015, two observation times are used homogeneously at around 10AM and 1:30PM local time. Before that period, only afternoon observations are used, which have been less stable in time, and afterwards only morning observations, as the afternoon observations strongly drifted in time.

For a comparison of the time series of the three high-level cloud type occurrences, we have analysed the JCHs of the updated GEWEX cloud assessment database, by applying the same CTP and COT thresholds as for this analysis and the results are shown in Figure 5.

The most stable CLARA-A3 relative amount is the one of maritime tropical high opaque clouds, of about 11%. The continental tropical relative high opaque cloud amount is nearly twice as large (about 18%) and less stable. The largest relative amount is the one of tropical cirrus, with 25% over ocean and 35% over land. The relative amount of tropical thin cirrus shows an opposite behaviour, with 20% over ocean and only 10% over land. These results are similar to the ones of MODIS-CLDPROP, though with larger absolute values, as the high-level cloud amount is about 15% larger for CLARA-A3. However, PATMOS-x, also based on AVHRR measurements, does not show any difference between tropical ocean and land for thin cirrus and high opaque clouds.
According to CLARA-A3, the relative amount of all three high-level cloud types increases since the 2000’s. However, this behaviour is not observed in the other datasets (PATMOS-x, MODIS-CLDPROP and AIRS-CIRS).

Anomaly Patterns linked to the Global Surface Temperature#

As it is difficult to observe cloud trends, in particular over a time period which is short for climate change attribution, we explore in this part of the notebook how regional patterns change with respect to interannual global surface temperature variability.

The global surface temperature reflects global warming, but large interannual changes are related to episodes of El Niño (warm) and La Niña (cool), as is illustrated in Figure 6, which presents the time series of deseasonalised global mean surface temperature anomalies (black curve, left axis) and the Southern Oscillation Index (SOI, right axis, with red and blue indicating El Niño and La Niña episodes, respectively). These data are obtained from the GISS Surface Temperature Analysis (for global mean surface temperature anomalies) and the NOAA website (for the SOI).

It is interesting to note that during the period before 2003 the global surface temperature anomalies are all negative, in particular between 1982 and 1992. Indeed, there has been a climate warming during the last four decades, but these negative anomalies may be partly explained by aerosol dimming [16] and the Mount Pinatubo eruption in 1991.

For the following analysis of regional cloud type variability patterns with respect to inter-annual global mean surface temperature anomalies, we concentrate on the period 2003-2015, for which the observation times of the CLARA-A3 JCHs are coherent.

CLARA-A3 shows a relative increase per °C of warming of all these three cloud types near the equator in the tropical Pacific and a decrease over Indonesia, similar to the cloud datasets in the updated GEWEX cloud assessment and similar the pattern caused by the El Niño–Southern Oscillation (ENSO [10]).

We also observe an increase in cirrus and thin cirrus in the subtropics. This may be in relation to a hypothesized widening of the Hadley cell with global warming [11].

The relative amount of thin cirrus also increases around the equator, close to the increase of high opaque clouds. This can be reconciled with the findings that tropical mesoscale convective systems get higher [12] and the area of surrounding thin cirrus gets larger with their height [13].

The different geographical change patterns of high opaque clouds and thin cirrus lead to variations in atmospheric heating and cooling gradients which then influence the large scale circulation, as has already been investigated [14].
