Production date: 13-05-2026

Produced by: Inês Girão e Luís Figueiredo (+ATLANTIC)

🌍 Use case: Characterising land cover in fire-burned areas#

❓ Quality assessment question#

  • How consistent are C3S Land Cover classes in burned areas when compared with independent burned-area and land-cover datasets?

Understanding how different datasets represent land cover within burned areas is essential for ensuring the reliability of fire impact assessments and downstream applications such as ecosystem monitoring and carbon accounting. Both land cover (LC) and burned area (BA) are recognized as Essential Climate Variables (ECVs), but differences in their derivation can lead to inconsistencies when they are analysed together.

In this notebook, we use three complementary datasets distributed via the Copernicus Climate Change Service (C3S) and by MapBiomas :

  • The Land Cover Classification Gridded Maps from 1992 to present derived from satellite observations (hereafter referred to as C3S LC). These maps, produced at 300 m spatial resolution, describe annual land cover states and transitions across 22 global classes, following the FAO/UN Land Cover Classification System. The dataset ensures temporal consistency by integrating multiple Earth observation missions, including AVHRR, SPOT-VGT, PROBA-V, and Sentinel-3 OLCI/SLSTR, and is designed to support climate modelling and environmental monitoring.

  • The Fire burned area from 2001 to present derived from satellite observations (hereafter referred to as C3S Fire),provides global burned area information. The product is based on MODIS surface reflectance and active fire detections and offers both pixel-level maps (250 m spatial resolution) with attributes such as date of the first detection, confidence level and land cover burned pixels and gridded products (0.25° resolution) with attributes such as burned area, standard error, fraction of obseserved and burnable area, number of patches and sum of burned area for each land cover category.

  • The MapBiomas Land Cover dataset, hereafter referred to as MapBiomas LC, and the MapBiomas Burned Area dataset, hereafter referred to as MapBiomas Fire, provide annual information on multiple variables, including land cover, land use and burned areas. Both products are generated primarily from Landsat imagery at 30 m spatial resolution through automated classification workflows supported by satellite mosaics, temporal analysis, machine-learning algorithms, region-specific rules, expert review and post-processing to improve spatial and temporal consistency. Together, they enable the assessment of long-term land-cover transitions, including deforestation, agricultural expansion, vegetation recovery and urban growth, as well as the characterisation of the vegetation types or land-use systems affected by fire.

This analysis focuses on evaluating the consistency of land cover information within burned areas across datasets. Specifically, we assess whether the land cover classes assigned to burned pixels in the Fire dataset are coherent with those reported in the MapBiomas Fire products.

📢 Quality assessment statement#

These are the key outcomes of this assessment

  • After harmonisation to IPCC land categories, C3S and MapBiomas show broadly consistent total land-cover composition over Brazil. Both products identify Forest as the dominant class, followed by Other and Agriculture.

  • The main differences in total land-cover composition are in the relative allocation of area between Forest, Agriculture and Other. MapBiomas assigns a larger share to Forest, while C3S assigns a larger share to Agriculture.

  • Land-cover composition within burned areas shows stronger product differences than total land-cover composition. This means that agreement at the full-area level does not automatically translate into the same class distribution when the analysis is restricted to burned pixels.

  • Despite these differences, both products show a coherent high-level pattern: burned areas are mainly associated with vegetation-related IPCC classes.

  • A key quality finding is that cross-product agreement improves in the more recent year analysed. Burned-area totals differ strongly in 2001 but are much closer in 2019, suggesting greater consistency for recent burned-area land-cover assessment.

  • The Other IPCC class remains the main interpretation caveat because it aggregates heterogeneous land-cover types. Differences involving Other should therefore be interpreted cautiously.

  • Overall, the results support the use of C3S Land Cover as a broad IPCC-level land-cover context for burned-area analysis, particularly in recent years. The comparison is not intended as detailed subclass validation.

Here we convert the ds_lc dataset that is indexed by a time coordinate into one indexed by year: it first adds a new coordinate year by extracting the calendar year from each timestamp in time, then replaces the time dimension with year across all variables and drops the original time variable. The following code cleans up the temporal dimension of the ds_fire dataset so that it has a consistent monthly index. It starts by creating a canonical month label using the start date from each time_bounds interval, ensuring that every timestamp is aligned to the first day of its month. It then removes duplicates by grouping on this new monthly label and keeping a single record per month (you can change the reducer—e.g. mean, sum, max—if you want a different way of combining duplicates). After that, it reindexes the dataset onto a continuous monthly timeline (freq="MS") from the first to the last available month, inserting NaNs where months are missing so that the gaps remain visible in analysis and plots. Finally, it adds simple integer year and month coordinates, making it easier to filter, group, or aggregate by calendar units.

# Subset
def subset_latlon(ds, minx, miny, maxx, maxy, lon_name="longitude", lat_name="latitude"):
    """Subset a dataset by bbox while respecting coordinate orientation."""
    lat_values = ds[lat_name].values
    lon_values = ds[lon_name].values

    lat_slice = slice(maxy, miny) if lat_values[0] > lat_values[-1] else slice(miny, maxy)
    lon_slice = slice(minx, maxx) if lon_values[0] < lon_values[-1] else slice(maxx, minx)
    return ds.sel({lon_name: lon_slice, lat_name: lat_slice})

# Area Caculation helper functions

R_EARTH_KM = 6371.0088
EXCLUDED_AREA_CLASSES = {"No data / Other"}
NODATA_VALUES = {0}


def grid_cell_area_by_lat(latitude, longitude, radius_km=R_EARTH_KM):
    """
    Return the area (km²) of one grid cell for each latitude row.

    The C3S LC and Fire products are in geographic coordinates. On this grid,
    the angular resolution is constant but the real pixel area decreases with
    latitude. This function uses the spherical quadrangle formula.
    """
    lat = np.asarray(latitude, dtype="float64")
    lon = np.asarray(longitude, dtype="float64")

    if lat.ndim != 1 or lon.ndim != 1:
        raise ValueError("latitude and longitude must be 1D coordinates")
    if lat.size < 2 or lon.size < 2:
        raise ValueError("at least two latitude and longitude values are required")

    dlat = float(np.nanmedian(np.abs(np.diff(lat))))
    dlon = float(np.nanmedian(np.abs(np.diff(lon))))

    lat_rad = np.deg2rad(lat)
    half_dlat_rad = np.deg2rad(dlat) / 2.0
    dlon_rad = np.deg2rad(dlon)

    return (radius_km ** 2) * dlon_rad * np.abs(
        np.sin(lat_rad + half_dlat_rad) - np.sin(lat_rad - half_dlat_rad)
    )


def raster_pixel_area_rows_km2(src, rows, radius_km=R_EARTH_KM):
    """
    Return the area (km²) of one pixel for each raster row in a rasterio window.
    Works for projected rasters and geographic rasters.
    """
    transform = src.transform

    if src.crs is not None and src.crs.is_projected:
        return np.full(len(rows), abs(transform.a * transform.e) / 1_000_000.0)

    dlon = abs(transform.a)
    dlat = abs(transform.e)
    lat_centers = transform.f + (rows + 0.5) * transform.e

    lat_rad = np.deg2rad(lat_centers)
    half_dlat_rad = np.deg2rad(dlat) / 2.0
    dlon_rad = np.deg2rad(dlon)

    return (radius_km ** 2) * dlon_rad * np.abs(
        np.sin(lat_rad + half_dlat_rad) - np.sin(lat_rad - half_dlat_rad)
    )


def summarise_class_area(class_area, exclude_classes=EXCLUDED_AREA_CLASSES):
    """
    Compute valid total and percentages, excluding no-data classes from the
    denominator. Excluded classes are retained in the returned area dictionary
    but receive 0% in the percentage dictionary.
    """
    exclude_classes = set(exclude_classes or [])
    valid_classes = [cls for cls in class_area if cls not in exclude_classes]
    total_area = float(sum(class_area[cls] for cls in valid_classes))

    percentages = {
        cls: (float(area) / total_area * 100.0) if (total_area > 0 and cls not in exclude_classes) else 0.0
        for cls, area in class_area.items()
    }
    return total_area, percentages


def clean_lc_array(arr, nodata_values=NODATA_VALUES):
    """Return a NumPy array and a valid-data mask for categorical LC rasters."""
    values = np.asarray(arr)
    valid = np.isfinite(values) if np.issubdtype(values.dtype, np.floating) else np.ones(values.shape, dtype=bool)

    if nodata_values:
        valid &= ~np.isin(values, list(nodata_values))

    return values, valid

# Function for Fire dataset LC area calculation
def burned_area_by_lc_c3s(
    ds,
    class_map,
    years_to_include=None,
    exclude_classes=EXCLUDED_AREA_CLASSES,
    nodata_values=NODATA_VALUES,
):
    """
    Compute burned area (km²) by aggregated C3S / FireCCI land-cover class.

    Memory-safe version:
    - selects burned pixels with JD > 0;
    - uses latitude-dependent pixel area;
    - avoids flattening all burned pixels into large temporary arrays;
    - processes each latitude row separately;
    - excludes no-data / QA classes from totals and percentages.

    Parameters
    ----------
    ds : xarray.Dataset
        Dataset with variables:
        - JD: burned date / Julian day. Burned pixels have JD > 0.
        - LC: land-cover class associated with burned pixels.
        Dimensions should include time, latitude, longitude.

    class_map : dict
        Mapping of aggregated classes to native C3S / FireCCI LC values.

    years_to_include : list or None
        Optional list of years to process.

    exclude_classes : set or list
        Aggregated classes excluded from totals and percentages.

    nodata_values : set or list
        Native LC values treated as invalid.

    Returns
    -------
    area_results    : dict {year: {class: area_km2}}
    percent_results : dict {year: {class: percentage}}
    total_results   : dict {year: valid_total_area_km2}
    """
    if years_to_include is not None:
        years_to_include = {int(y) for y in years_to_include}

    time_years = np.array([
        pd.Timestamp(t).year
        for t in ds["time"].values
    ])

    selected_time_indices = np.where(
        np.isin(time_years, list(years_to_include))
    )[0]

    if len(selected_time_indices) == 0:
        raise ValueError(
            f"No C3S Fire timesteps found for years {sorted(years_to_include)}. "
            f"Available years are {sorted(np.unique(time_years))}."
        )

    ds = ds.isel(time=selected_time_indices)

    print(
        "Selected C3S Fire timesteps:",
        [str(pd.Timestamp(t).date()) for t in ds["time"].values]
    )

    # Latitude-dependent area for one pixel in each latitude row.
    area_per_lat = grid_cell_area_by_lat(
        ds["latitude"].values,
        ds["longitude"].values,
    )

    # Convert class lists to numpy arrays once.
    class_values_map = {
        class_name: np.asarray(class_values)
        for class_name, class_values in class_map.items()
    }

    nodata_values = set(nodata_values or [])

    yearly_class_area = {}
    times = ds["time"].values

    for i, t in enumerate(times):
        timestamp = pd.Timestamp(t)
        year = int(timestamp.year)

        print(
            f"Processing C3S Fire {timestamp.date()} ({i + 1}/{len(times)})",
            end="\r",
        )

        if year not in yearly_class_area:
            yearly_class_area[year] = {
                class_name: 0.0
                for class_name in class_map.keys()
            }

        # Load only one timestep.
        # np.asarray prevents keeping xarray wrappers around the array.
        jd = np.asarray(ds["JD"].sel(time=t).values)
        lc = np.asarray(ds["LC"].sel(time=t).values)

        jd = np.squeeze(jd)
        lc = np.squeeze(lc)

        if jd.ndim != 2 or lc.ndim != 2:
            raise ValueError(
                f"Expected 2D JD and LC arrays for time {t}, "
                f"got JD shape {jd.shape}, LC shape {lc.shape}"
            )

        if jd.shape != lc.shape:
            raise ValueError(
                f"JD and LC shapes do not match for time {t}: "
                f"{jd.shape} vs {lc.shape}"
            )

        # Process row by row to avoid large flattened temporary arrays.
        n_rows = lc.shape[0]

        for row_idx in range(n_rows):
            jd_row = jd[row_idx, :]
            lc_row = lc[row_idx, :]

            # Burned pixels only.
            row_mask = jd_row > 0

            if nodata_values:
                for nodata_value in nodata_values:
                    row_mask &= lc_row != nodata_value

            if not np.any(row_mask):
                continue

            burned_lc_row = lc_row[row_mask]
            pixel_area_km2 = area_per_lat[row_idx]

            for class_name, class_values in class_values_map.items():
                count = np.count_nonzero(np.isin(burned_lc_row, class_values))
                if count:
                    yearly_class_area[year][class_name] += float(
                        count * pixel_area_km2
                    )

        del jd, lc

    print("\nDone.")

    area_results = {}
    percent_results = {}
    total_results = {}

    for year, class_dict in sorted(yearly_class_area.items()):
        total_area, percentages = summarise_class_area(
            class_dict,
            exclude_classes=exclude_classes,
        )

        year_str = str(year)
        area_results[year_str] = class_dict
        total_results[year_str] = total_area
        percent_results[year_str] = percentages

    return area_results, percent_results, total_results

# Function for MapBiomas Fire dataset LC area calculation
def burned_area_by_lc_biomas(
    year_path_map,
    class_map,
    exclude_classes=EXCLUDED_AREA_CLASSES,
    nodata_values={0},
):
    """
    Compute MapBiomas Fire burned area (km²) by land-cover / land-use class.

    This memory-safe version:
    - expects local cached raster paths, not remote URLs;
    - reads the raster block by block;
    - calculates pixel area by raster row;
    - avoids loading the full raster into memory;
    - avoids building large full-raster boolean masks.

    Parameters
    ----------
    year_path_map : dict
        Mapping {year: local_path_to_mapbiomas_fire_tif}.

    class_map : dict
        Mapping of aggregated classes to native MapBiomas class values.

    exclude_classes : set or list
        Aggregated classes excluded from valid totals and percentages.

    nodata_values : set or list
        Native raster values treated as invalid.

    Returns
    -------
    area_results    : dict {year: {class: area_km2}}
    percent_results : dict {year: {class: percentage}}
    total_results   : dict {year: valid_total_area_km2}
    """
    area_results = {}
    percent_results = {}
    total_results = {}

    years = sorted(year_path_map.keys(), key=lambda y: int(y))

    # Convert class values once.
    class_values_map = {
        cls_name: np.asarray(cls_values)
        for cls_name, cls_values in class_map.items()
    }

    for i, year in enumerate(years):
        print(f"Processing MapBiomas Fire {year} ({i + 1}/{len(years)})")

        raster_path = year_path_map[year]
        class_area = {cls_name: 0.0 for cls_name in class_map.keys()}

        with rasterio.open(raster_path) as src:
            current_nodata_values = set(nodata_values or [])
            if src.nodata is not None:
                current_nodata_values.add(src.nodata)

            for _, window in src.block_windows(1):
                data = src.read(1, window=window)

                # Skip empty / nodata-only windows quickly.
                valid_data = np.ones(data.shape, dtype=bool)
                for nodata_value in current_nodata_values:
                    valid_data &= data != nodata_value

                if not valid_data.any():
                    del data, valid_data
                    continue

                # Pixel area by row for this window.
                rows = np.arange(
                    window.row_off,
                    window.row_off + window.height,
                )
                pixel_area_rows = raster_pixel_area_rows_km2(src, rows)

                # Count class pixels per row and multiply by row area.
                for cls_name, cls_values in class_values_map.items():
                    class_mask = valid_data & np.isin(data, cls_values)

                    if not class_mask.any():
                        del class_mask
                        continue

                    pixels_per_row = np.count_nonzero(class_mask, axis=1)
                    class_area[cls_name] += float(
                        np.sum(pixels_per_row * pixel_area_rows)
                    )

                    del class_mask, pixels_per_row

                del data, valid_data, pixel_area_rows

        total_area, percentages = summarise_class_area(
            class_area,
            exclude_classes=exclude_classes,
        )

        year_str = str(int(year))
        area_results[year_str] = class_area
        total_results[year_str] = total_area
        percent_results[year_str] = percentages

    print("Done.")
    return area_results, percent_results, total_results

def lulc_area_by_class_c3s(
    ds,
    class_map,
    years_to_include=None,
    exclude_classes=EXCLUDED_AREA_CLASSES,
):
    """
    Compute C3S LULC area by class.

    This version avoids keeping unnecessary large temporary arrays and uses
    row-wise area weighting for the geographic 300 m grid.
    """
    if years_to_include is not None:
        years_to_include_str = {str(y) for y in years_to_include}
        ds = ds.sel(year=[y for y in ds["year"].values if str(int(y)) in years_to_include_str])

    area_per_lat = grid_cell_area_by_lat(
        ds["latitude"].values,
        ds["longitude"].values,

    percent_results = {}
    total_results = {}

    for i, year in enumerate(years):
        print(f"Processing C3S LULC {int(year)} ({i + 1}/{len(years)})")

        lc = ds["lccs_class"].sel(year=year).values
        lc = np.asarray(np.squeeze(lc))

        if lc.ndim != 2:
            raise ValueError(
                f"Expected a 2D LC array for year {year}, got shape {lc.shape}"
            )

        valid_lc = np.ones(lc.shape, dtype=bool)
        for nodata_value in NODATA_VALUES:
            valid_lc &= lc != nodata_value

        class_area = {class_name: 0.0 for class_name in class_map.keys()}

        for class_name, class_values in class_map.items():
            class_mask = valid_lc & np.isin(lc, class_values)

            # Count pixels per latitude row, then apply latitude-dependent area.
            pixels_per_row = np.count_nonzero(class_mask, axis=1)
            class_area[class_name] = float(np.sum(pixels_per_row * area_per_lat))

            class_area,
            exclude_classes=exclude_classes,
        )

        year_str = str(int(year))
        area_results[year_str] = class_area
        total_results[year_str] = total_area
        percent_results[year_str] = percentages

        del lc, valid_lc

    print("Done.")
    return area_results, percent_results, total_results

# Plotting Helper functions
def _canonical_class_name(name):
    """
    Normalize class names so that color lookup and exclusion are robust across
    similar naming conventions.

    Examples:
    - Forest / Forest Land -> forest
    - Agriculture / Cropland -> agriculture
    - Other / Other Land / Sparse vegetation / Bare -> other
    """
    if name is None:
        return None

    raw = str(name).strip()
    norm = raw.lower().strip()

    alias_map = {
        "forest": "forest",
        "forest land": "forest",

        "agriculture": "agriculture",
        "cropland": "agriculture",

        "grassland": "grassland",

        "wetlands": "wetlands",
        "wetland": "wetlands",

        "settlements": "settlements",
        "settlement": "settlements",
        "urban": "settlements",
        "urban / built-up": "settlements",
        "urban/built-up": "settlements",
        "built-up": "settlements",

        "other": "other",
        "other land": "other",
        "sparse / bare": "other",
        "sparse vegetation / bare": "other",
        "sparse vegetation": "other",
        "bare": "other",

        "water": "water",

        "no data": "no data",
        "no data / other": "no data",
        "excluded / no data": "no data",
        "no data / excluded": "no data",
        "not observed": "no data",
    }

    return alias_map.get(norm, raw)


def _resolve_class_color(cls, class_colors, default="#cccccc"):
    """
    Return a class color using:
    1. exact class-name match;
    2. canonicalized class-name match;
    3. default fallback.
    """
    if class_colors is None:
        return default

    if cls in class_colors:
        return class_colors[cls]

    cls_canon = _canonical_class_name(cls)

    if cls_canon in class_colors:
        return class_colors[cls_canon]

    for key, value in class_colors.items():
        if _canonical_class_name(key) == cls_canon:
            return value

    return default


def _text_color_for_fill(fill_color):
    """
    Choose black or white text depending on fill luminance.
    """
    try:
        r, g, b = mcolors.to_rgb(fill_color)
        luminance = 0.2126 * r + 0.7152 * g + 0.0722 * b
        return "#222222" if luminance > 0.62 else "white"
    except Exception:
        return "white"


def _is_excluded(cls, exclude_classes):
    """
    Check whether a class should be excluded, using both exact and canonical names.
    """
    if not exclude_classes:
        return False

    exclude_exact = set(exclude_classes)
    exclude_canon = {_canonical_class_name(x) for x in exclude_classes}

    return (cls in exclude_exact) or (_canonical_class_name(cls) in exclude_canon)


def _available_classes(results_a, results_b):
    """
    Collect the union of all classes that appear in either result dictionary
    across all years.
    """
    available = set()

    for results in [results_a, results_b]:
        for class_dict in results.values():
            available.update(class_dict.keys())

    return available


def _classes_for_plot(results_a, results_b, class_order, exclude_classes=None):
    """
    Return a stable class order for two result dictionaries.

    Uses the union of classes found in either dataset.
    Preserves the order given in class_order.
    Any extra classes not listed in class_order are appended at the end.
    """
    available = _available_classes(results_a, results_b)

    ordered = [
        cls for cls in class_order
        if cls in available and not _is_excluded(cls, exclude_classes)
    ]

    extras = sorted([
        cls for cls in available
        if cls not in ordered and not _is_excluded(cls, exclude_classes)
    ])

    return ordered + extras


def _format_years(results_a, results_b):
    """
    Return sorted common years from two result dictionaries.

    Keeps the original year-key type used in the dictionaries, but sorts
    numerically when possible.
    """
    years_a = set(results_a.keys())
    years_b = set(results_b.keys())
    years = list(years_a & years_b)

    if not years:
        raise ValueError(
            "No common years found between the two result dictionaries. "
            f"Dataset A years: {sorted(years_a)}; Dataset B years: {sorted(years_b)}"
        )

    try:
        return sorted(years, key=lambda y: int(y))
    except Exception:
        return sorted(years)


def _bar_width(n):
    """
    Return a visually reasonable bar width depending on the number of years.
    """
    if n <= 1:
        return 0.45
    if n == 2:
        return 0.60
    if n <= 5:
        return 0.70
    return 0.80


def _add_custom_legend(ax, classes, class_colors):
    """
    Add a custom legend using the same class colors as the stacked bars.
    """
    handles = [
        mpatches.Patch(
            facecolor=_resolve_class_color(cls, class_colors),
            label=cls,
            linewidth=0,
        )
        for cls in classes
    ]

    legend = ax.legend(
        handles=handles,
        loc="upper left",
        bbox_to_anchor=(1.01, 1),
        frameon=False,
        fontsize=9.5,
        handlelength=1.2,
        handleheight=1.2,
        borderpad=0,
        labelspacing=0.55,
    )

    for text in legend.get_texts():
        text.set_color("#444444")


def _results_to_plot_percent(results, classes, years):
    """
    Convert an area-results dictionary into percentages normalized over the
    visible classes only.

    This guarantees that each plotted stacked bar sums to 100% over the
    classes shown in the plot.
    """
    out = {}

    for year in years:
        values = {
            cls: float(results[year].get(cls, 0.0))
            for cls in classes
        }

        denominator = sum(max(v, 0.0) for v in values.values())

        if denominator <= 0:
            out[year] = {cls: 0.0 for cls in classes}
        else:
            out[year] = {
                cls: 100.0 * max(values[cls], 0.0) / denominator
                for cls in classes
            }

    return out


def _totals_from_results(results, classes, years):
    """
    Calculate totals for each year using only the displayed classes.
    """
    totals = {}

    for year in years:
        totals[year] = sum(
            float(results[year].get(cls, 0.0))
            for cls in classes
        )

    return totals


# Summary table function
def area_totals_table_generic(
    results_a,
    results_b,
    class_order,
    exclude_classes=None,
    label_a="Dataset A",
    label_b="Dataset B",
):
    """
    Small QA table to confirm comparable totals for a pair of area result
    dictionaries.

    Totals are calculated only over the classes displayed after applying
    class_order and exclude_classes.
    """
    years = _format_years(results_a, results_b)

    classes = _classes_for_plot(
        results_a,
        results_b,
        class_order,
        exclude_classes=exclude_classes,
    )

    rows = []

    for year in years:
        total_a = sum(float(results_a[year].get(cls, 0.0)) for cls in classes)
        total_b = sum(float(results_b[year].get(cls, 0.0)) for cls in classes)

        rows.append({
            "year": int(year) if str(year).isdigit() else year,
            f"{label_a} total km²": total_a,
            f"{label_b} total km²": total_b,
            "absolute difference km²": abs(total_a - total_b),
            "relative difference %": (
                abs(total_a - total_b) / total_a * 100.0
                if total_a
                else np.nan
            ),
        })

    return pd.DataFrame(rows)

def plot_area_comparison_generic(

    dataset_labels=("Dataset A", "Dataset B"),
    exclude_classes=None,
    y_label="Area (km²)",
):
    """
    Plot two area-result dictionaries side by side using a custom class order
    and color map.
    """
    classes = _classes_for_plot(
        results_a,
        results_b,
        class_order,
        exclude_classes=exclude_classes,
    )

    if not classes:
        raise ValueError(
            "No classes available for plotting after applying class_order "
            "and exclude_classes."
        )

    years = _format_years(results_a, results_b)
    n = len(years)

    totals_a = _totals_from_results(results_a, classes, years)
    totals_b = _totals_from_results(results_b, classes, years)

    global_max = max(
        max(totals_a.values()) if totals_a else 0.0,
        max(totals_b.values()) if totals_b else 0.0,
    )

    fig, axes = plt.subplots(
        1,
        2,
        figsize=(max(12, n * 2 + 6), 6),
        sharey=True,
    )
    fig.patch.set_facecolor("#ffffff")

    x = np.arange(n)
    width = _bar_width(n)

    for ax, data, totals, label in [
        (axes[0], results_a, totals_a, dataset_labels[0]),
        (axes[1], results_b, totals_b, dataset_labels[1]),
    ]:
        ax.set_facecolor("#f8f8f6")
        ax.spines[["top", "right", "left"]].set_visible(False)
        ax.spines["bottom"].set_color("#cccccc")
        ax.tick_params(
            axis="both",
            which="both",
            length=0,
            labelsize=10,
            colors="#555555",
        )
        ax.yaxis.grid(True, color="#e0e0e0", linewidth=0.7, zorder=0)
        ax.set_axisbelow(True)

        bottom = np.zeros(n, dtype=float)

        for cls in classes:
            values = np.array(
                [float(data[y].get(cls, 0.0)) for y in years],
                dtype=float,
            )
            color = _resolve_class_color(cls, class_colors)

            bars = ax.bar(
                x,
                values,
                width=width,
                bottom=bottom,
                color=color,
                linewidth=0.4,
                edgecolor="white",
                zorder=3,
            )

            for bar, val in zip(bars, values):
                # Label only medium/large segments.
                if global_max > 0 and (val / global_max) > 0.035:
                    ax.text(
                        bar.get_x() + bar.get_width() / 2,
                        bar.get_y() + bar.get_height() / 2,
                        f"{val:,.0f}",
                        ha="center",
                        va="center",
                        fontsize=7,
                        color=_text_color_for_fill(color),
                        fontweight="bold",
                        clip_on=True,
                    )

            bottom += values

        for i, year in enumerate(years):
            total = totals[year]
            ax.text(
                x[i],
                total + global_max * 0.01 if global_max > 0 else 0.02,
                f"{total:,.0f}",
                ha="center",
                va="bottom",
                fontsize=9,
                color="#444444",
            )

        ax.set_ylim(0, global_max * 1.15 if global_max > 0 else 1)
        ax.set_xticks(x)
        ax.set_xticklabels(years, fontsize=10)
        ax.yaxis.set_major_formatter(FuncFormatter(lambda v, _: f"{v:,.0f}"))
        ax.set_title(
            label,
            fontsize=12,
            fontweight="bold",
            color="#222222",
            pad=10,
            loc="left",
        )

        if n == 1:
            ax.set_xlim(-0.6, 0.6)

    axes[0].set_ylabel(y_label, fontsize=10, color="#555555", labelpad=10)
    axes[1].tick_params(axis="y", left=False, labelleft=False)

    fig.suptitle(
        title,
        fontsize=13,
        fontweight="bold",
        color="#222222",
        x=0.02,
        ha="left",
        y=1.01,
    )

    _add_custom_legend(axes[1], classes, class_colors)

    fig.tight_layout()
    plt.show()

Analysis of total land-cover composition#

The total land-cover area plots compare the distribution of IPCC land categories across Brazil for C3S LC and MapBiomas LC in 2001 and 2019.

Both products show that Forest is the dominant land-cover class in Brazil in both years. However, the relative proportion of forest differs between products. MapBiomas estimates a larger forest share, decreasing from approximately 65.8% in 2001 to 62.2% in 2019. C3S estimates a lower forest share, decreasing from approximately 52.7% in 2001 to 51.4% in 2019.

The difference between the products is also visible in the Agriculture class. C3S assigns a substantially larger proportion of the country to Agriculture, increasing from approximately 23.6% in 2001 to 24.8% in 2019. MapBiomas shows a lower Agriculture share, increasing from approximately 8.1% in 2001 to 11.2% in 2019.

These differences are expected because the two products use different input data, spatial resolutions, classification systems, and class definitions.

Despite these differences, both products show a consistent broad pattern between 2001 and 2019:

  • Forest remains the dominant class.

  • Agriculture increases over time.

  • Forest area decreases over time.

Analysis of land-cover composition within burned areas#

The burned-area land-cover plots show the IPCC land-cover composition of pixels identified as burned. In this analysis, the burned-area layer is used as a spatial mask, and the land-cover classes are then summarized only within that burned-pixel subset.

The distribution within burned areas differs from the total land-cover distribution. In both MapBiomas and C3S, burned pixels are concentrated in vegetation-related IPCC classes rather than in Settlements or No data. This indicates that both products provide a plausible broad land-cover context for burned-area analysis.

For MapBiomas, Forest is the largest burned-area class in both years, accounting for approximately 43.6% of burned area in 2001 and 41.3% in 2019. Other, Wetlands, and Grassland also contribute substantially, indicating that burned areas are distributed across several natural or semi-natural vegetation categories.

For C3S, the burned-area composition is different. The dominant class is Other, accounting for approximately 49.9% of burned area in 2001 and 46.5% in 2019. Forest is lower than in MapBiomas but increases from approximately 22.1% in 2001 to 29.1% in 2019. Agriculture also contributes a notable share, representing approximately 16.0% in 2001 and 14.8% in 2019.

The total burned area also differs between the two product families. MapBiomas decreases from approximately 222,228 km² in 2001 to 193,912 km² in 2019, while C3S increases from approximately 140,248 km² to 198,130 km². As a result, the total burned-area estimates are much closer in 2019 than in 2001.

The strongest difference in class composition concerns the balance between Forest and Other. This is important because Other is a heterogeneous category that may include shrubland, sparse vegetation, bare areas, water-related classes, and product-specific classes depending on the source product. Results involving Other should therefore be interpreted cautiously.

Overall, the burned-area plots show that both products identify burned areas mainly within vegetation-related IPCC classes, supporting the broad plausibility of the comparison. However, the relative allocation of burned pixels across Forest, Other, Agriculture, Grassland, and Wetlands differs between products.

6. Main takeaways#

  • IPCC harmonisation makes the C3S and MapBiomas products thematically comparable by grouping their original land-cover legends into the same broad reporting classes.

  • For total land-cover composition, C3S and MapBiomas are broadly consistent after IPCC aggregation. Both products identify Forest as the dominant class, followed by Other and Agriculture.

  • The main differences in total land-cover composition are in the balance between Forest, Agriculture and Other. MapBiomas assigns a larger proportion of the study area to Forest, while C3S assigns a larger proportion to Agriculture.

  • Differences become more pronounced when the analysis is restricted to land cover within burned areas.

  • Both products consistently show that burned areas are mainly associated with vegetation-related IPCC categories, supporting the broad plausibility of the C3S land-cover context for burned-area analysis.

  • Cross-product agreement is better in 2019 than in 2001, especially for the total burned area. This suggests that recent-year burned-area land-cover assessments are more robust than early-period comparisons.

  • The Other class should be interpreted cautiously because it aggregates several heterogeneous land-cover types, including shrubland, sparse vegetation, bare areas, water-related classes and product-specific categories.
